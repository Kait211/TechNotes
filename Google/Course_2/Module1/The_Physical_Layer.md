-  The physical layer consists of ​devices and means of ​transmitting bits across computer networks. 
- A **bit** is the smallest representation of data that a computer can understand: its a one or a zero

### Modulation
A way of varying the voltage of this charge moving across the cable 

### Twisted pair cable
The most common type of cabling used for connecting computer devices 

### Duplex communication
The concept that information can flow in both directions across the cable 

### Simplex communication
The process is unidirectional 
- devices on either side of a networking link can ​both communicate with each other at the exact same time. ​This is known as **full duplex**. ​If there's something wrong with the connection, ​you might see a network link degrade and ​report itself as operating as half-duplex. **​Half-duplex** means that while ​communication is possible in each direction, ​only one device can be communicating at a time. 

- **RJ45 (Registered Jack 45):** The most common connector used for **Ethernet network cables**.
- **Network Port:** A physical port where an Ethernet cable plugs into a device like a **switch, router, server, or desktop**.
- **Switches:** Have many network ports to connect multiple devices.
- **Link Light (LED):** Indicates a **successful physical connection** between two powered-on devices.
- **Activity Light (LED):** Flashes when **data is being transmitted** over the network.
- **Port LEDs:** Can help **troubleshoot network connection issues** and may also indicate **link speed**.
- **Patch Panel:** A device that **organizes and terminates network cables** but **does not forward network traffic**.
- **Patch Panels connect** wall cables to **switches or routers**, providing network access to connected devices.

As we have covered, ports and patch panels are part of the Physical Layer. Cabling tools are an expansion of the Physical Layer. In this reading, you will learn more about cabling tools, what they look like, and what they are used for.

**Crimper**  
A crimper is a hand-held tool that looks like a set of pliers. It is used to squeeze down or crimp wires.

**Cable Stripper**  
A cable stripper is another handheld device that also looks like pliers. Its purpose is to remove the protective rubber coating from cables.

**Wi-Fi Analyzer**  
A Wi-Fi analyzer is a scanner that analyzes the power and quality of the Wi-Fi in an area. It also collects data about the Wi-Fi and its surroundings.

**Toner Probe**  
A toner probe is part of a set of devices used for finding Ethernet and other network cables. One device plugs into a cable, while the other device, the toner probe, emits a tone that gets louder as it gets closer to the connected cable (Meyers, 2022).

**Punch Down Tool**  
A punch down tool, also known as a Krone tool, is used for punching wires into patch panels or jacks. First, the protective covering is removed from the wires, and then the wires are punched into place.

**Cable Tester**  
A cable tester digitally measures the integrity of a cable for compliance with established cabling standards. It measures several parameters:

- Attenuation
- Impedance
- Noise
- Near-End Crosstalk (NEXT)
- Attenuation to Crosstalk Ratio (ACR)
- PowerSum NEXT (Network Encyclopedia, 2022)

**Loopback Plug**  
A loopback plug is a device used to test ports. Wires are connected in a loop that sends traffic back to the port after it receives it.

**Network Tap**  
A network tap is an external device that copies network traffic for use by monitoring devices.
